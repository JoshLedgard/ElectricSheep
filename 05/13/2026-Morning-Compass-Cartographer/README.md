# Morning Compass Cartographer · May 13, 2026

**What it is:** A pocket cartography toy. Tap to stamp three to five small landmarks — grove, well, bridge, hearth, lantern — onto a soft paper map. Drag the compass rose in the corner to choose a heading. Tap *Chart route* to watch a dotted loop unfold, then copy a tiny postcard naming the path and the morning it makes.

**Inspiration:** The thematic seed is fresh starts and morning curiosity — the small ritual of imagining a short walk before the day actually begins. Cartographic visual language (deckled-edge paper, hatch-shaded woodland, contour rings, a compass rose) was chosen because maps are inherently optimistic objects: they assume you will go somewhere. No personal sources, conversations, or private details were used.

**How to open:** Open `index.html` in any modern browser. Self-contained, no server, no installs, no network.

**Controls:**
- Tap a landmark stamp (Grove, Well, Bridge, Hearth, Lantern) to arm it, then tap the map to drop one. Up to five marks; tap a stamp again to disarm.
- Drag the compass rose (top-right of the map) to rotate it; the bearing changes which landmark the route begins at.
- *Chart route*: draws a smooth dotted loop through your landmarks in compass order and writes the postcard.
- *Random*: lays down a fresh scene with a random bearing and an instantly charted loop.
- *Reset*: clears all landmarks and returns the compass to north.
- *Copy postcard*: copies the named route, the route line, and the prose to the clipboard.

**Why it was built:** Because a clean morning often feels like a tiny mapmaking exercise — picking three or four small places, then sketching a path between them. The toy turns that little mental walk into something you can rotate and re-chart in under a minute. It is meant to be quiet, replayable, and finished before the kettle boils.

**Privacy check:** No real names, addresses, phone numbers, emails, dollar amounts, credentials, exact quoted text, or other private personal details appear in the page, the source, or the generated postcards. Place names are composed from generic abstract word lists (e.g., *Mossy Crossing*, *Tin Hollow*). The page makes no network requests and uses no external assets or fonts; everything renders from inline SVG and CSS. Clipboard writes happen only when the user presses *Copy postcard*.
